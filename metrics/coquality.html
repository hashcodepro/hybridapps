Testing Fundamentals

Cost Of Quality

Definition

Cost of Quality (COQ) is a measure that quantifies the cost of control/conformance and the cost of failure of control/non-conformance. In other words, it sums up the costs related to prevention and detection of defects and the costs due to occurrences of defects.

  1. Definition by ISTQB: cost of quality: The total costs incurred on quality activities and issues and often split into prevention costs, appraisal costs, internal failure costs and external failure costs.
  2. Definition by QAI: Money spent beyond expected production costs (labor, materials, equipment) to ensure that the product the customer receives is a quality (defect free) product. The Cost of Quality includes prevention, appraisal, and correction or repair costs.

Explanation

  1. Cost of Control (Also known as Cost of Conformance)
    1. Prevention Cost
      1. The cost arises from efforts to prevent defects.
      2. Example: Quality Assurance costs
    2. Appraisal Cost
      1. The cost arises from efforts to detect defects.
      2. Example: Quality Control costs
  2. Cost of Failure of Control (Also known as Cost of Non-Conformance)
    1. Internal Failure Cost
      1. The cost arises from defects identified internally and efforts to correct them.
      2. Example: Cost of Rework (Fixing of internal defects and re-testing)
    2. External Failure Cost
      1. The cost arises from defects identified by the client or end-users and efforts to correct them.
      2. Example: Cost of Rework (Fixing of external defects and re-testing) and any other costs due to external defects (Product service/liability/recall, etc)

Formula / Calculation

Cost of Quality (COQ) = Cost of Control + Cost of Failure of Control

where

Cost of Control = Prevention Cost + Appraisal Cost

and

Cost of Failure of Control = Internal Failure Cost + External Failure Cost

Notes

  1. In its simplest form, COQ can be calculated in terms of effort (hours/days).
  2. A better approach will be to calculate COQ in terms of money (converting the effort into money and adding any other tangible costs like test environment setup).
  3. The best approach will be to calculate COQ as a percentage of total cost. This allows for comparison of COQ across projects or companies.
  4. To ensure impartiality, it is advised that the Cost of Quality of a project/product be calculated and reported by a person external to the core project/product team (Say, someone from the Accounts Department).
  5. It is desirable to keep the Cost of Quality as low as possible. However, this requires a fine balancing of costs between Cost of Control and Cost of Failure of Control. In general, a higher Cost of Control results in a lower Cost of Failure of Control. But, the law of diminishing returns holds true here as well.
